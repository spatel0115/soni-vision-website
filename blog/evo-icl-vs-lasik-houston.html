LASIK & EVO ICL

EVO ICL vs. LASIK:
Which Is Best for You?

NR
Dr. Nikitha Reddy, MD
February 2025  ·  7 min read

The question I get most often from patients considering vision correction surgery is some version of this: "Should I get LASIK or EVO ICL?" It's a great question — and the honest answer is that it depends entirely on your eyes.

Both procedures eliminate the need for glasses and contacts. Both are safe, effective, and performed routinely at Soni Vision Institute. But they work in fundamentally different ways, and the right choice for you depends on factors that only a thorough evaluation can reveal.

Here's how I walk my patients through the decision.

How LASIK Works

LASIK (Laser-Assisted In Situ Keratomileusis) reshapes the cornea — the clear dome of tissue at the front of the eye — to change how it bends light. During the procedure, a femtosecond laser creates a thin flap in the cornea. An excimer laser then removes microscopic amounts of corneal tissue to correct your refractive error. The flap is repositioned and heals naturally, without stitches.

The whole procedure takes about 10 minutes per eye. Most patients see 20/20 or better by the morning after surgery. Recovery is fast — the majority of patients return to work within 24 to 48 hours.

LASIK is the world's most-performed elective surgical procedure. Over 700,000 procedures are performed in the United States each year, with a patient satisfaction rate consistently above 96%.

How EVO ICL Works

EVO ICL (Implantable Collamer Lens) takes a completely different approach. Instead of reshaping the cornea, a small lens is implanted inside the eye — between the iris and the natural crystalline lens — to correct your prescription from within.

The lens is made of Collamer, a biocompatible material derived from collagen that integrates naturally with the eye's environment. It is invisible inside the eye, provides UV protection, and requires no maintenance. Unlike LASIK, EVO ICL doesn't remove any corneal tissue at all.

The procedure takes about 20 to 30 minutes and is performed under topical anesthesia. Vision typically stabilizes within the first week, and most patients return to normal activities within a few days.

"EVO ICL doesn't make you a LASIK patient who wasn't qualified — it gives you results that are often better than what LASIK achieves."

The Key Differences

Understanding the distinctions helps clarify which option fits your situation:

  • Corneal tissue: LASIK permanently removes corneal tissue. EVO ICL removes none.
  • Reversibility: LASIK results are permanent (the cornea cannot be un-reshaped). EVO ICL can be removed and replaced if your prescription changes significantly over time.
  • Prescription range: LASIK treats prescriptions from approximately -8.00 to +6.00 D. EVO ICL can treat prescriptions up to -20.00 D — far beyond LASIK's reach.
  • Dry eye: LASIK can worsen existing dry eye. EVO ICL does not affect tear production.
  • Visual quality: Many patients report slightly better visual quality with EVO ICL — higher contrast, better night vision — because the optics are inside the eye rather than on the corneal surface.

Who Is a LASIK Candidate?

LASIK is an excellent option if you meet the following criteria:

  • Prescription between -8.00 and +6.00 D
  • Adequate corneal thickness (enough tissue to safely reshape)
  • Stable prescription for at least 1 to 2 years
  • Age 18 or older (most commonly performed between 20 and 45)
  • No significant dry eye disease
  • No irregular corneal shape (e.g., keratoconus)

If all of these apply to you, LASIK is likely a safe and effective option that will deliver excellent results.

Who Should Consider EVO ICL Instead?

EVO ICL is the right choice if you:

  • Have a prescription higher than -8.00 D
  • Have thin corneas that don't qualify for LASIK
  • Have moderate to severe dry eye disease
  • Have been told previously that you're "not a LASIK candidate"
  • Prefer the option of a reversible procedure
  • Want the highest possible visual quality, particularly at night

I want to be very direct about this: being told you're not a LASIK candidate is not a dead end. EVO ICL was developed specifically for patients in this situation. Many of my most grateful patients are those who came to us after being turned away elsewhere — and are now glasses-free.

Cost: Is There a Significant Difference?

Both procedures represent an investment in permanent vision correction, and the costs are comparable. LASIK typically runs $2,000 to $3,500 per eye depending on the technology used. EVO ICL is in a similar range. Neither is typically covered by insurance as an elective procedure, though HSA/FSA funds can generally be used for both.

When you consider the lifetime cost of glasses, contacts, contact lens solutions, and eye exams, both procedures typically pay for themselves within a few years.

How We Make the Decision Together

At your consultation, we perform a comprehensive evaluation that includes corneal topography, pachymetry (corneal thickness measurement), refraction, and a dry eye assessment. Based on those measurements — combined with your prescription, lifestyle, and priorities — we give you an honest, personalized recommendation.

We offer both procedures and have no financial incentive to push you toward one or the other. Our only interest is finding the option that gives you the best outcome for your specific eyes.
