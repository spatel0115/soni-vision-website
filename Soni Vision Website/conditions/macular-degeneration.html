Eye Conditions

Macular
Degeneration

Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a leading cause of vision loss in adults over 50. Early detection and ongoing monitoring are essential to preserve your central vision.

Understanding the Condition

What Is
Macular Degeneration?

AMD affects the macula — the central part of the retina responsible for sharp, straight-ahead vision used for reading, driving, and recognizing faces. As the macula deteriorates, central vision becomes blurry, distorted, or absent, while peripheral vision typically remains intact.

There are two forms: dry AMD (more common), in which the macula gradually thins over years; and wet AMD (more aggressive), in which abnormal blood vessels grow beneath the retina and can cause rapid vision loss. Early detection through regular eye exams and retinal imaging is the single most important factor in preserving long-term vision.

Signs to Watch For

Symptoms of
Macular Degeneration

AMD often progresses without noticeable symptoms in its early stages. Know what to watch for — and schedule regular monitoring if you are over 50 or have a family history.

01

Blurry or Distorted Central Vision

Straight lines that appear wavy or bent — a symptom known as metamorphopsia — is a hallmark warning sign of AMD, particularly the wet form.

02

Dark or Empty Areas in Vision

A central blind spot or smudge in the visual field — most noticeable when reading or looking at a face — indicates significant macular damage.

03

Colors Appear Faded

The vibrancy and contrast of colors diminish as the macular photoreceptors responsible for color discrimination are affected.

04

Difficulty Recognizing Faces

Since face recognition relies on central vision, AMD patients often report difficulty identifying people — even those they know well.

Management & Treatment

How We
Manage AMD

While dry AMD has no cure, its progression can be slowed. Wet AMD, when caught early, responds well to anti-VEGF therapy — which can halt or even partially reverse vision loss. Early diagnosis is everything.

Regular Monitoring & OCT Imaging

Optical coherence tomography (OCT) provides detailed cross-sectional images of the retina, allowing us to detect early changes and track progression with precision.

Anti-VEGF Injections (Wet AMD)

Medications such as Eylea, Lucentis, and Avastin block the growth of abnormal blood vessels. When initiated promptly, they are highly effective at preserving — and sometimes improving — vision.

Lifestyle Modifications & Supplements

The AREDS2 supplement formula has been shown to reduce the risk of AMD progression in intermediate and advanced cases. Smoking cessation, diet, and UV protection also play a meaningful role.

Common Questions

Macular Degeneration
FAQ

What our patients most want to understand about AMD and long-term vision preservation.


Ask Us Directly
Is macular degeneration curable?

Currently, there is no cure for AMD. However, there are important distinctions: dry AMD progresses slowly and can be managed with nutritional supplements and lifestyle changes that meaningfully slow progression. Wet AMD, which is more aggressive, can be effectively treated with anti-VEGF injections that stop abnormal vessel growth — and in many patients, regular treatment stabilizes vision for years. The earlier AMD is identified, the better the long-term outcome.

How often should I be monitored?

If you are over 50 with no AMD, annual comprehensive eye exams are appropriate. If you have early dry AMD, we typically recommend monitoring every 6 to 12 months with OCT imaging. Intermediate or advanced AMD warrants more frequent monitoring — often every 3 to 6 months. Wet AMD being treated with injections is monitored monthly or every 2 months depending on the treatment protocol. We will establish the right schedule based on your individual findings.
