Eye Conditions

Dry Eye
Syndrome

Dry eye syndrome is a chronic condition affecting millions of Americans. When the eyes don't produce enough quality tears, the result is discomfort, blurry vision, and potential surface damage. Effective treatment is available.

Understanding the Condition

What Is
Dry Eye Syndrome?

Dry eye occurs when the tear film is unstable or insufficient — leaving the eye surface exposed, irritated, and vulnerable to damage. The tear film is a complex three-layer structure: an outer oily layer, a middle aqueous layer, and an inner mucus layer. A deficiency in any layer can produce dry eye disease.

Common contributing factors include aging, hormonal changes (particularly in women post-menopause), prolonged screen use, air conditioning, certain medications (antihistamines, antidepressants, diuretics), contact lens wear, and systemic conditions such as Sjogren's syndrome and rheumatoid arthritis.

Signs to Watch For

Symptoms of
Dry Eye

Dry eye symptoms range from mildly annoying to severely disruptive. They often fluctuate throughout the day and worsen in specific environments.

01

Burning or Stinging Sensation

A persistent burning, stinging, or scratching sensation — particularly in dry indoor environments, after screen time, or in windy conditions.

02

Gritty or Foreign Body Feeling

The sensation that something is in the eye — sand, debris, or a foreign object — even when nothing is physically present.

03

Excessive Tearing (Reflex Tearing)

Paradoxically, dry eye can cause watery eyes. The eye overproduces reflex tears in response to surface irritation — but these tears lack the stabilizing oils of a healthy tear film.

04

Blurry Vision (Especially When Reading)

Vision fluctuates and improves transiently with blinking — a characteristic pattern of tear-film instability that makes sustained near tasks difficult.

Treatment Options

How We
Treat Dry Eye

Dry eye is a chronic disease — it requires ongoing management rather than a single cure. We work with each patient to build a personalized treatment plan based on the severity and underlying cause of their dry eye.

Artificial Tears & Lubricating Drops

High-quality preservative-free artificial tears remain the first line of treatment for mild to moderate dry eye, supplementing the natural tear film and providing immediate relief.

Punctal Plugs

Tiny biocompatible plugs inserted into the tear drainage canals to slow tear drainage and increase tear volume on the ocular surface. A simple, reversible in-office procedure.

Prescription Drops (Restasis / Xiidra)

Anti-inflammatory prescription eye drops that address the underlying inflammatory component of chronic dry eye disease — reducing symptoms and improving tear production over time.

Lifestyle Modifications

Omega-3 supplementation, warm compresses, eyelid hygiene, reduced screen time, humidifier use, and avoiding environmental triggers can substantially reduce dry eye burden.

Common Questions

Dry Eye
FAQ

What our patients most want to know about dry eye and its management.


Ask Us Directly
Can dry eyes be permanently cured?

In most cases, dry eye is a chronic condition that requires ongoing management rather than a one-time cure. However, many patients achieve excellent long-term symptom control with the right combination of treatments — to the point where dry eye is no longer a meaningful quality-of-life concern. For some patients whose dry eye is caused by a specific, reversible factor (such as a medication that can be changed), addressing that root cause can provide lasting improvement.

Does LASIK cause dry eye?

LASIK can temporarily reduce corneal sensation and decrease tear production in the months following surgery, which may worsen pre-existing dry eye or produce new dry eye symptoms. This is typically transient — resolving within 3 to 6 months as corneal nerves regenerate. Patients with significant pre-existing dry eye may be advised to consider PRK or EVO ICL instead, as these procedures have a lower associated risk of post-operative dry eye. We evaluate dry eye status thoroughly at every LASIK consultation before making a recommendation.
