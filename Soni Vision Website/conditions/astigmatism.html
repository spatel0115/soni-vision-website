Eye Conditions

What Is
Astigmatism?

Astigmatism is a common and treatable imperfection in the curvature of the eye — causing blurred or distorted vision at all distances. Correctable with glasses, contacts, or surgery.

Understanding the Condition

What Is
Astigmatism?

The cornea or lens has an irregular shape — more like a football than a basketball — preventing light from focusing evenly on the retina. Instead of a single sharp focal point, light is scattered across two or more focal points, producing blurred or distorted vision at any distance.

Astigmatism is extremely common and often occurs alongside nearsightedness (myopia) or farsightedness (hyperopia). It can be present from birth or develop gradually over time. Fortunately, it is one of the most reliably correctable refractive errors in eye care.

Signs to Watch For

Symptoms of
Astigmatism

Astigmatism symptoms can be subtle or pronounced — and many people don't realize they have it until a comprehensive eye exam.

01

Blurred or Distorted Vision

Objects appear smeared, doubled, or out of focus at all distances — not just near or far. Straight lines may appear tilted or wavy.

02

Eye Strain & Headaches

The eyes constantly work to compensate for the irregular focus, leading to fatigue, tension headaches, and discomfort after sustained visual tasks.

03

Poor Night Vision

Glare and halos around lights are more pronounced in low-light conditions, making nighttime driving particularly challenging.

04

Difficulty Reading

Text may appear blurry or letters may seem to run together, requiring extra effort to read — even with corrective lenses.

Treatment Options

How We
Treat Astigmatism

Most cases of astigmatism can be fully corrected — either with non-surgical options or with permanent surgical procedures that eliminate the need for glasses or contacts.

Toric Contact Lenses

Specially designed soft lenses with different powers in different meridians to compensate for the irregular corneal curvature.

LASIK or PRK

Laser vision correction that reshapes the cornea to eliminate astigmatism permanently. Both procedures are highly effective for most prescriptions.

Toric IOL (with Cataract Surgery)

When cataract surgery is indicated, a toric intraocular lens corrects astigmatism at the same time — reducing or eliminating the need for glasses post-surgery.

Refractive Lens Exchange

For patients who are not LASIK candidates or prefer a permanent lens-based solution, RLE replaces the natural lens with a toric IOL.

Common Questions

Astigmatism
FAQ

Answers to the questions we hear most often about astigmatism and its treatment.


Ask Us Directly
Can astigmatism worsen over time?

In most adults, astigmatism is stable. However, certain conditions — such as keratoconus, a progressive thinning of the cornea — can cause astigmatism to worsen significantly. A comprehensive eye exam and corneal topography can identify whether your astigmatism is stable or changing, which is an important factor in determining the right treatment.

Is LASIK safe for astigmatism?

Yes — LASIK and PRK are FDA-approved to correct astigmatism and are among the most thoroughly studied elective procedures in medicine. Most patients with astigmatism are excellent LASIK candidates. The key is a thorough preoperative evaluation, including corneal mapping, to confirm the cornea is healthy and thick enough for treatment. Our surgeons perform this evaluation at your initial consultation.
