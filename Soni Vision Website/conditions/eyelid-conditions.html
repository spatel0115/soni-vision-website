Eye Conditions

Eyelid
Conditions

Eyelid conditions range from cosmetic concerns to conditions that directly impair vision. Our surgeons provide evaluation and treatment for a range of eyelid-related issues affecting both comfort and function.

Understanding the Conditions

The Role of
Healthy Eyelids

The eyelids play a critical role in protecting the eye and maintaining a healthy tear film. They spread tears evenly across the ocular surface with every blink, shield the eye from debris and light, and drain tears efficiently through the lacrimal system.

When eyelids are affected by ptosis, dermatochalasis, blepharitis, or malposition conditions such as ectropion or entropion, both vision and eye health can suffer. Some eyelid conditions are purely cosmetic; others are medically significant and may qualify for insurance coverage. Our team evaluates each patient individually to determine the best path forward.

Common Eyelid Conditions

Conditions We
Evaluate & Treat

A range of eyelid conditions can affect vision, comfort, and eye health. Here are the most common presentations we see.

01

Ptosis (Drooping Eyelid)

The upper eyelid droops lower than normal due to weakness or detachment of the levator muscle. In significant cases, the lid obstructs the visual axis — impairing vision and causing a characteristic chin-up head posture.

02

Dermatochalasis (Excess Eyelid Skin)

Age-related laxity causes excess upper or lower eyelid skin to fold and hang, sometimes obstructing peripheral and superior vision. Blepharoplasty restores a natural, open appearance and, when vision is affected, may be covered by insurance.

03

Blepharitis (Eyelid Inflammation)

Chronic inflammation of the eyelid margins — often associated with bacteria, Demodex mites, or meibomian gland dysfunction — causing redness, crusting, irritation, and worsening dry eye symptoms.

04

Ectropion / Entropion (Eyelid Malposition)

Ectropion (outward-turning lid) causes chronic tearing and exposure. Entropion (inward-turning lid) allows lashes to rub against the cornea, causing pain and potential corneal scarring. Both are corrected surgically.

Treatment Options

How We
Treat Eyelid Conditions

Treatment depends on the specific condition and its severity. Some eyelid issues are managed medically; others require surgical correction. Our team will recommend the most appropriate approach after a thorough evaluation.

Surgical Correction (Ptosis Repair, Blepharoplasty)

Ptosis repair tightens or re-attaches the levator muscle to raise the eyelid. Upper lid blepharoplasty removes excess skin and, when vision is compromised, is often covered by Medicare or insurance after visual field testing confirms the obstruction.

Medical Management (Warm Compresses, Antibiotics)

Blepharitis and meibomian gland dysfunction respond well to a consistent warm compress and lid hygiene routine, often combined with topical or oral antibiotics when infection is present.

Referral to Oculoplastics Specialist as Needed

Complex eyelid malpositions, orbital conditions, or cases requiring advanced reconstructive techniques are referred to a fellowship-trained oculoplastic surgeon — with a warm handoff and coordinated follow-up care.

Common Questions

Eyelid Conditions
FAQ

What patients most want to understand before their eyelid evaluation.


Ask Us Directly
Is eyelid drooping (ptosis) covered by insurance?

Ptosis repair is typically covered by Medicare and most insurance plans when the drooping eyelid is causing a functional visual obstruction. This is determined through visual field testing, which quantifies the degree to which the lid encroaches on the visual field. Cosmetic ptosis correction — performed purely for appearance without functional visual impairment — is not covered. We will document the medical necessity carefully to support your insurance claim and explain exactly what to expect before moving forward.

When does eyelid skin become a medical concern?

Excess upper eyelid skin (dermatochalasis) becomes a medical concern when it hangs over the lashes and obstructs the superior or peripheral visual field. Patients often describe having to raise their brows or tilt their head back to see clearly, and fatigue from this compensatory effort can be significant. When visual field testing confirms functional obstruction, upper lid blepharoplasty may qualify for insurance coverage. We perform this assessment in-office and will advise you on your eligibility after the evaluation.
