Eye Conditions

Hyperopia
(Farsightedness)

Hyperopia causes difficulty seeing objects up close, and in severe cases, blurry vision at all distances. Several surgical options can permanently correct hyperopia.

Understanding the Condition

What Is
Hyperopia?

The eyeball is too short, or the cornea has too little curvature — causing incoming light to focus behind the retina rather than directly on it. Mild hyperopia may be compensated by the eye's natural focusing ability, but this leads to fatigue and strain over time.

In moderate to severe cases, vision is blurry at all distances. Young patients with hyperopia often have an uncanny ability to compensate through accommodation — but this ability diminishes with age, making the condition increasingly symptomatic over time. Surgical correction eliminates the need for constant visual effort.

Signs to Watch For

Symptoms of
Hyperopia

Farsightedness is often underdiagnosed because younger patients compensate unconsciously — until the effort catches up with them.

01

Blurry Near Vision

Reading, phone use, and close work are blurry or require significant effort to focus. Nearby objects appear soft or unclear.

02

Eye Strain & Fatigue

The eyes are constantly overworking to compensate for insufficient optical power, leading to a persistent sense of visual effort and tiredness.

03

Frequent Headaches

Tension-type headaches — particularly around the forehead and brow — that occur during or after near-vision tasks are a hallmark of uncorrected hyperopia.

04

Difficulty with Close Work

Reading, sewing, computer work, and other near-focus tasks become fatiguing or uncomfortable, especially in the afternoon or evening.

Treatment Options

How We
Treat Hyperopia

Hyperopia can be corrected non-surgically or eliminated permanently through surgery. The right approach depends on the degree of farsightedness, age, and whether the patient is also experiencing presbyopia.

LASIK or PRK

Laser vision correction reshapes the corneal curvature to improve the eye's refractive power. Highly effective for mild to moderate hyperopia with a healthy, appropriately sized cornea.

Refractive Lens Exchange

For patients with moderate to high hyperopia — or those over 45 — replacing the natural lens with a premium multifocal or EDOF IOL corrects both farsightedness and presbyopia simultaneously.

Reading Glasses or Contacts (Non-Surgical)

Prescription glasses and toric contact lenses remain a reliable, non-invasive option for patients who prefer to defer surgery or are not surgical candidates.

Common Questions

Hyperopia
FAQ

Answers to the most common questions about farsightedness and surgical correction.


Ask Us Directly
Is hyperopia hereditary?

Yes — farsightedness has a strong genetic component. If one or both parents are farsighted, children are more likely to inherit the condition. However, the degree of hyperopia can vary significantly even within families. Regular eye exams starting in childhood are important for early detection, since children with significant hyperopia may develop amblyopia (lazy eye) if left untreated.

Can LASIK fix farsightedness?

Yes — LASIK and PRK are FDA-approved for the correction of hyperopia, typically up to +6.00 diopters. The laser reshapes the central cornea to increase its curvature and improve the eye's refractive power. For higher degrees of farsightedness, or for patients over 40 who also have presbyopia, refractive lens exchange with a premium IOL often provides a more complete and lasting result. Your consultation will determine which option is most appropriate.
