Cataract Surgery

How to Choose a Cataract Surgeon
in the Houston Area

RS
Dr. Ruhi Soni, MD
January 2025  ·  6 min read

Every week, I meet patients who were referred to me by their optometrist, their family physician, or a neighbor who had surgery with us. What surprises me is how many of them had never really considered that they had a choice — that cataract surgeons differ meaningfully in training, technique, outcomes, and the care they provide.

You do have a choice. And for an elective procedure that will permanently change your vision, that choice matters.

Here are the five questions I believe every patient should ask before trusting a surgeon with their eyes.

1. Is the Surgeon Fellowship-Trained?

All ophthalmologists complete residency training in eye surgery — but not all complete a fellowship. A fellowship is an additional year or two of advanced, subspecialty training beyond residency, focused on a specific area such as anterior segment surgery (cataracts, cornea) or refractive surgery (LASIK, EVO ICL).

Fellowship training matters for several reasons:

  • Fellows train under surgeons who perform far higher volumes of complex cases than typical residency programs
  • Fellowship-trained surgeons are more equipped to handle complications — thin posterior capsules, dense cataracts, small pupils, prior LASIK — because they've managed these in training
  • The advanced biometry techniques and IOL calculation methods for complex cases are typically fellowship-level skills

Both Dr. Nikitha Reddy and I completed fellowship training in anterior segment and refractive surgery. It's not required to be a good surgeon — but in ophthalmology, it's a meaningful signal.

2. Will the Same Surgeon Perform Every Step of My Care?

In large, high-volume ophthalmology practices, the reality is that you may meet your surgeon once — and then be passed to a resident, a nurse practitioner, or a different physician for follow-up care. This isn't inherently bad, but it creates discontinuity that matters when something unexpected happens.

At a surgical-only practice like ours, you work directly with your surgeon from the initial consultation through all post-operative appointments. That means if you develop a question about your recovery at 10pm, you can message us. If your vision isn't progressing as expected at your one-week check, the same surgeon who operated on you is evaluating the outcome.

"The best surgical outcome doesn't start in the operating room — it starts with an honest conversation in the exam room."

3. What Premium IOL Options Do They Offer — and How Do They Choose?

Cataract surgery is not just about removing the clouded lens. The lens that replaces it — the intraocular lens (IOL) — determines the quality of your vision for the rest of your life.

Not every practice offers all premium IOL types. And even among practices that do, the quality of the recommendation process varies enormously. What you want is a surgeon who:

  • Offers the full range: monofocal, toric, multifocal, extended depth-of-focus (EDOF), and light-adjustable lenses
  • Uses advanced biometry to take precise measurements before recommending a lens
  • Has a structured process for matching the right lens to each patient's lifestyle and anatomy — not a one-size-fits-all default
  • Is honest about trade-offs: multifocal lenses, for example, can cause temporary halos. A good surgeon explains this upfront rather than overselling the outcome

If a surgeon talks you through only one or two IOL options, or gives you the impression there's only one right choice — that's a sign to ask more questions.

4. What Technology Do They Use?

Modern cataract surgery is extraordinarily precise, but the tools make a real difference. When evaluating a practice, ask about:

  • Biometry platform: Advanced biometry devices (such as the IOLMaster 700 or Lenstar) measure the eye with high accuracy, which is critical for IOL power calculation
  • Femtosecond laser capability: Laser-assisted cataract surgery enables more precise incisions and lens fragmentation, which can reduce the energy used inside the eye
  • Corneal topography: Essential for detecting subtle corneal irregularities and planning astigmatism correction
  • Intraoperative aberrometry: Real-time measurement of the eye's optical characteristics during surgery, allowing IOL power refinement in the operating room

You don't need to memorize these. But a surgeon who proactively mentions the technology they use and why — without being asked — is usually one who cares deeply about precision.

5. What Do Their Actual Patients Say?

Google reviews are imperfect, but they're the most transparent window we have into a practice's patient experience. Look for:

  • Volume: A practice with 500+ reviews tells a different story than one with 40
  • Recency: Are reviews coming in consistently, or are there long gaps?
  • Specificity: Reviews that mention specific surgeons by name, describe the outcome in detail, or comment on follow-up care are more reliable than generic praise
  • Responses: How the practice responds to both positive and negative reviews reveals a great deal about their character

Soni Vision Institute holds a 4.9-star rating across more than 703 Google reviews — the highest of any eye surgery practice in Greater Houston. We're proud of that, but more importantly, I invite you to read the reviews themselves. They'll tell you more than any marketing copy ever could.

A Note on Convenience vs. Quality

Houston has dozens of practices offering cataract surgery. Some are large, high-volume chains with multiple locations — convenient, certainly. Others are smaller, surgical-focused practices where every patient gets direct access to their surgeon and individualized attention.

The right choice depends on what you value. If your primary concern is logistics, a large multi-location practice might work. But if you want a surgeon who knows your name, remembers your history, and is personally invested in your outcome — a smaller, surgical-only practice is worth seeking out.

Cataract surgery is one of the safest procedures in medicine. But "safe" and "optimal" are not the same thing. You deserve both.
