Advanced Glaucoma Surgery

Glaucoma Surgery,
Minimized.

Minimally Invasive Glaucoma Surgery (MIGS) represents the next generation of glaucoma treatment — offering the pressure-lowering benefits of traditional surgery with a dramatically safer profile and faster recovery. Often combined with cataract surgery.

Next-Generation Glaucoma Care

What Is
MIGS?

MIGS procedures use microscopic instruments and tiny incisions — often through the same wound used for cataract surgery — to access and improve the eye's natural drainage pathways. By working with the eye's existing anatomy rather than creating new drainage channels, MIGS achieves meaningful IOP reduction with a significantly lower complication profile than conventional filtering surgery.

Dr. Nikitha Reddy is fellowship-trained in advanced MIGS procedures, having trained under world-renowned glaucoma surgeon Dr. Ike Ahmed at the University of Toronto — one of the foremost MIGS training programs in the world. She offers the full spectrum of MIGS technologies to patients throughout Greater Houston.

Microscopic Incisions

MIGS devices are implanted through incisions measured in fractions of a millimeter — far smaller than those used in conventional glaucoma surgery.

Lower Risk vs. Traditional Surgery

The safety profile of MIGS is dramatically better than trabeculectomy or tube shunt surgery — fewer complications, less recovery time, and preserved surgical options for the future.

Often Combined with Cataract Surgery

Many MIGS procedures are performed simultaneously with cataract surgery — treating two conditions at once with a single visit, a single recovery, and no additional incisions.

Faster Recovery

Most MIGS patients recover on the same timeline as cataract surgery alone — returning to normal activities within days, not weeks.

MIGS Procedures Offered

Advanced MIGS
Technologies

Dr. Reddy offers the full range of current-generation MIGS procedures. The right choice depends on the severity of your glaucoma, your IOP targets, and whether cataract surgery is being performed simultaneously.

01

iStent Inject

The iStent inject places two microscopic titanium stents into the trabecular meshwork — creating permanent bypass channels that improve aqueous humor outflow and reduce IOP. Commonly combined with cataract surgery.

02

Kahook Dual Blade

The Kahook Dual Blade performs an ab interno goniotomy — excising a precise strip of trabecular meshwork tissue to improve drainage. Effective for mild to moderate open-angle glaucoma, with or without cataract surgery.

03

Hydrus Microstent

The Hydrus Microstent is an 8mm flexible scaffold implanted into Schlemm's canal, expanding and scaffolding the canal to dramatically improve outflow over a wide area. Indicated for mild to moderate open-angle glaucoma combined with cataract surgery.

04

Combined Cataract + MIGS

For patients with both cataracts and glaucoma, performing MIGS at the time of cataract removal is a highly efficient approach — one surgery, one recovery, and two conditions treated simultaneously. Coverage and eligibility depend on glaucoma severity and insurance.

Ask Us Directly
How is MIGS different from traditional glaucoma surgery?

Traditional glaucoma surgeries — trabeculectomy and tube shunt procedures — create entirely new drainage pathways outside the eye's natural anatomy. They are highly effective at lowering IOP but carry significant risks including infection, hypotony (excessively low pressure), scarring, and prolonged recovery. MIGS procedures, by contrast, work within the eye's existing drainage anatomy using sub-millimeter instruments. This results in a substantially lower complication rate, faster recovery, and preserved options for more aggressive surgery in the future if needed. The trade-off is that MIGS typically achieves more modest IOP reduction — making it most appropriate for mild to moderate glaucoma.

Can MIGS replace eye drops?

For many patients, MIGS significantly reduces the number of glaucoma drops required, and some patients are able to discontinue drops entirely after surgery. Clinical data on combined cataract-MIGS procedures consistently shows meaningful drop reduction at 12–24 months post-operatively. Whether you can eliminate drops completely depends on your baseline IOP, your target pressure (determined by optic nerve damage severity), and your individual response to the procedure. Dr. Reddy will set realistic expectations during your consultation based on your specific situation.

Who is a candidate for MIGS?

MIGS is most appropriate for patients with mild to moderate open-angle glaucoma who have inadequate IOP control on drops, or who are undergoing cataract surgery and wish to address glaucoma simultaneously. Patients with advanced glaucoma, very high IOP targets, or certain types of glaucoma (such as narrow-angle or neovascular glaucoma) may be better served by more aggressive surgical approaches. A comprehensive glaucoma evaluation — including visual field testing, OCT nerve analysis, and IOP documentation — is necessary to determine candidacy.
