# Soni Vision Institute — Design Principles

## Brand Positioning
Premium boutique surgical practice. Two fellowship-trained ophthalmologists. Houston's highest-rated eye surgery team (4.95 stars). Surgical-only — not a glasses shop, not a chain.

**Benchmark:** Maloney-Shamie Vision Institute (maloneyshamievision.com) — sophisticated, high-end, surgical. Not clinical or corporate.

## Core Principles

### 1. Premium Over Busy
White space is not wasted space — it signals quality. Every section should breathe. When in doubt, add space, not content.

### 2. Physician-First
The doctors are the product. Dr. Soni and Dr. Reddy should be front and center on every page. Lead with the people, not the services.

### 3. Trust Signals Everywhere
4.95 stars is the best in all of Houston. This should be visible and prominent. Never bury it. Fellowship-trained, board-certified, surgical-only — these differentiators belong in the hero, not the footer.

### 4. Restrained Color Use
Navy and teal are accents, not backgrounds for entire sections (except the hero and CTA). Most of the site should be white or off-white. Color draws the eye — use it to highlight, not fill.

### 5. Serif for Drama, Sans for Clarity
Cormorant Garamond (display) creates the premium surgical feel. Inter (body) ensures readability. Never use the display font for body copy. Never use Inter for hero headings.

### 6. Mobile Is a First-Class Experience
50%+ of medical searches happen on mobile. Every layout decision must work at 390px. No horizontal scrolling. No cramped text. No squished photos.

### 7. CTAs Are a Conversation, Not a Demand
"Schedule a Consultation" — warm, not aggressive. Teal button on dark background. Outline button as secondary. One primary CTA per section max.

## What to Avoid
- Clip art or generic stock icons
- Too many competing CTAs on one screen
- Text blocks wider than 680px (hard to read)
- Light gray text on white (accessibility fail)
- Cramped section padding (min 72px mobile, 110px desktop)
- Nav items wrapping to multiple lines
- Photos that are too small to read faces
